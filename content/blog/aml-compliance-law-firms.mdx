Law firms occupy an unusual position in the financial system. They hold client funds, facilitate property transactions, structure corporate vehicles, and — in doing so — become potential conduits for money laundering. Regulators know this. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) has classified law firms as **designated non-financial businesses and professions (DNFBPs)** for well over a decade, and domestic AML frameworks in most jurisdictions have followed suit.

The practical consequence: your firm is not simply a professional services provider that handles money as a side effect of legal work. Under most frameworks, you carry standalone AML obligations that apply regardless of whether you have ever encountered a suspicious client.

## What Triggers the Obligations

The AML obligations that apply to law firms are generally activity-triggered rather than entity-triggered. You are subject to the rules when your firm:

- Receives, holds, or transmits client funds on behalf of a client
- Assists a client in buying, selling, or transferring real property
- Facilitates the formation, management, or operation of a legal entity or arrangement
- Assists in the management of client assets
- Acts as a nominee shareholder or director

In practice, this covers most transactional work: real estate closings, M&A, immigration bond matters, estate administration, and any matter where client funds pass through your trust account with a destination beyond legal fees.

## The Core Programme Requirements

A compliant AML programme for a law firm has five components that must be documented in writing, not simply practised informally.

**Client risk assessment.** Before accepting instructions, your firm must assess the AML risk profile of the prospective client — their identity, the nature of the intended transaction, the source of funds involved, and any adverse media or sanctions exposure. Most firms classify clients into three tiers (low, medium, high risk) that determine the level of due diligence required.

**Standard and enhanced due diligence.** Every client must be subject to standard due diligence: verification of identity and, for entities, verification of beneficial ownership (typically defined as natural persons holding more than 25% of equity or control). High-risk clients — those in sanctioned jurisdictions, politically exposed persons, or those involved in unusually complex transactions — require enhanced due diligence: additional documentation, senior sign-off, and ongoing monitoring.

**Suspicious activity monitoring and reporting.** Your firm must have a documented process for identifying and escalating suspicious activity. Staff must know who to report to (typically a designated Money Laundering Reporting Officer or MLRO), how quickly they must report, and what constitutes a reportable suspicion. Submitting a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) to the relevant financial intelligence unit is mandatory when the threshold for reasonable suspicion is met — and tipping off the client that a SAR has been filed is a separate criminal offence.

**Annual AML training.** All fee-earners and staff who handle client matters or funds must receive AML training at least annually. The training must cover the current regulatory requirements, your firm's internal policies, and the practical warning signs of suspicious activity relevant to your practice areas. Training records must be maintained.

**Independent review.** Most frameworks require periodic independent review of your AML programme — either by an external adviser or by someone within the firm who has no day-to-day compliance responsibilities. The review should assess whether your programme is fit for purpose given current risk exposure, not simply whether it exists.

## The Enforcement Trend

Bar associations and legal regulators are increasingly co-ordinating with financial intelligence units to audit law firm AML compliance. Enforcement actions in recent years have produced significant fines — not primarily for knowingly facilitating money laundering, but for failing to have a documented programme, failing to conduct the required due diligence, and failing to file SARs within the required timeframe.

The pattern is consistent: firms that cannot produce documentation of their client risk assessments, their EDD procedures on high-risk matters, or their training records are exposed to regulatory sanction even when there is no evidence of actual money laundering involvement.

## What This Means for Your Trust Account

The trust account sits at the intersection of AML compliance and accounting. Every deposit into your trust account should be traceable to a matter, a client, and a documented source-of-funds verification. Unexplained or poorly documented trust receipts are red flags under both bar examination standards and AML frameworks.

The practical standard we recommend: before accepting a significant trust deposit from a client, your file should contain documentation of who the funds came from, the underlying transaction they relate to, and why the funds are being held in trust rather than paid directly to a third party. This documentation protects your firm both in a bar examination and in an AML audit.
