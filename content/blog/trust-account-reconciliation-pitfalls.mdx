Every state bar requires law firms to maintain detailed, accurate, and current records of client funds held in trust. The standard is unambiguous. Yet trust account violations remain among the most common grounds for bar discipline — and in the vast majority of cases we review, the underlying cause is not theft or intentional misconduct. It is a bookkeeping shortcut, a misunderstood procedure, or a process that worked fine for a two-attorney firm and quietly broke down as the practice grew.

Here are the five reconciliation mistakes we encounter most frequently, and what each one actually means in a bar examination.

## 1. Reconciling to the Bank Balance Rather Than to the Client Ledger Sum

This is the most prevalent error we see, and it is the one that looks most harmless on the surface.

The **three-way reconciliation** required by virtually every state bar works like this:

1. Your bank statement balance must reconcile to your **trust ledger balance** (adjusted for outstanding deposits and outstanding cheques).
2. Your trust ledger balance must equal the **sum of all individual client ledger balances**.

Most firms perform step one. Fewer firms systematically perform step two.

The consequence: a firm can appear to have a balanced trust account at the bank-statement level while individual client balances are wrong — overdrawn, understated, or simply untracked. When a bar examiner asks you to reconcile Matter 2,847 to the trust ledger, and no individual client ledger exists for that matter, the firm is in violation regardless of whether the bank statement balanced.

**What to do:** Your reconciliation process must produce a listing of all individual client ledger balances, summed, and confirmed to equal the total trust ledger balance. This confirmation must be documented every month.

## 2. Using Trust Funds to Cover Overhead While Awaiting Earned Fee Recognition

In cash-flow-constrained periods, the temptation to temporarily draw fees from trust before a billing cycle closes is real. The logic — "the fees are earned, we'll recognise them in billing at the end of the month" — is flawed in two important ways.

First, funds in trust are not yours until they are properly earned and withdrawn following your jurisdiction's disbursement rules. Drawing them before that point is commingling, regardless of your intent or your accounting treatment.

Second, if a client disputes a bill or requires a refund after you have already drawn the funds, you may be unable to return them — which creates a shortage that triggers mandatory reporting obligations under most bar rules.

**What to do:** Establish a clear, documented policy for when fees are considered earned and when trust-to-operating transfers are permissible. In most jurisdictions, this means billing the client, receiving their approval (express or implied), and then executing a transfer documented in the client ledger.

## 3. Negative Client Balances

A client ledger balance cannot go negative. A negative balance in a client ledger means the firm has disbursed more funds on behalf of that client than it has received — which means it has either disbursed another client's funds, drawn operating funds through trust (impermissible commingling), or made an error.

Negative client ledger balances are a per se violation in most jurisdictions. Bar examiners specifically look for them. Yet in our experience, approximately 30% of firms that have never had a bar examination find at least one instance of a historical negative balance when they conduct a retroactive review.

**What to do:** Your reconciliation software should flag any negative client ledger balance as an error, not a warning. Investigate the cause before closing the reconciliation.

## 4. Keeping Earned Fees and Retainers in the Same Trust Account

General retainers — fees paid for the firm's general availability, which are earned upon receipt — do not belong in trust. Only **advance fee deposits** (fees paid before services are rendered, which remain the client's property until earned) must be held in trust.

The distinction matters. Firms that deposit general retainers into trust create compliance problems: the firm's own money is commingled with client funds, the trust balance overstates client liabilities, and the reconciliation becomes difficult to interpret.

Separately, flat-fee retainers present a specific trap. In most jurisdictions, a flat fee is not earned simply because the client paid it — it is earned as the work progresses, meaning a portion must remain in trust until the corresponding work is complete. Depositing a flat fee retainer into operating immediately is typically impermissible even if your engagement letter characterises it as "earned upon receipt."

**What to do:** Review your retainer typology with your bar's ethics guidance. Confirm that your trust account deposit procedures match the actual legal character of the funds you receive.

## 5. Failing to Promptly Disburse Unclaimed Balances

Client funds remaining in trust after a matter closes — unused cost advances, unearned fee portions returned to the client, uncashed settlement cheques — must be handled according to your jurisdiction's unclaimed property laws. Simply leaving them in trust indefinitely is not compliant.

Most jurisdictions require you to make reasonable efforts to contact the client, document those efforts, and — after a specified period — remit the unclaimed funds to the state's unclaimed property programme. Firms that allow small balances to accumulate in trust accounts over years are typically holding unclaimed property they are legally required to have remitted.

**What to do:** Run a quarterly report of trust account balances on closed matters. Establish a procedure for reaching out to clients with residual balances and document the outcome. For funds you cannot return, follow your jurisdiction's unclaimed property procedures exactly.
