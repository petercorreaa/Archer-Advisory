Law firms spend considerable time choosing their practice management platform and comparatively little time thinking about billing. This is backwards. Your practice management system organises your work. Your billing system determines how much of that work you actually get paid for.

The average law firm writes off between 12% and 18% of its billed time, and collects on average 85 to 90 cents of every dollar it does bill. The gap between work done and money received is real, and a meaningful portion of it is attributable to friction in the billing process — delays between time entry and invoice delivery, clunky client billing portals, LEDES compliance issues on insurance-defense matters, and manual collections follow-up that falls through the cracks.

Here is how we evaluate billing software when advising law firms on a platform choice or transition.

## Non-Negotiable: Native Three-Way Reconciliation Support

If you hold client funds in trust — and if you handle any advance fee deposits or cost retainers, you do — your billing platform must maintain matter-level trust ledger balances that reconcile to your bank account.

The single most expensive integration problem we encounter is firms that use one system for billing and a separate system for trust accounting with no native sync between them. Every trust disbursement must be manually entered in both places. Every fee transfer requires a manual journal entry. Reconciliation takes days instead of hours. Errors accumulate.

The platforms that handle this well natively — Cosmolex, Clio Payments, MyCase's built-in trust module — maintain the trust ledger within the same system as the billing and time entry records. When a cost is disbursed from trust, it appears in both the trust ledger and the matter billing file simultaneously. This is not a luxury feature; it is a compliance requirement dressed as a convenience.

If you are evaluating a platform that does not natively support three-way reconciliation, the cost of your external trust accounting process should be explicitly included in your total cost of ownership analysis.

## LEDES E-Billing: A Real Requirement for Insurance Defence Work

If any portion of your practice involves insurance-defense or corporate-panel work, you almost certainly submit invoices via LEDES (Legal Electronic Data Exchange Standard) e-billing to a client billing system — Bottomline Legal eXchange, TyMetrix, Legal Tracker, or similar.

LEDES compliance means your billing software must:

- Generate valid LEDES 98B or LEDES XML files without manual reformatting
- Support the client's specific billing code requirements (UTBMS task codes and activity codes)
- Accommodate line-item budget tracking, fee cap enforcement, and pre-approval workflows when required by the client's billing guidelines

Firms that handle LEDES billing in a platform not designed for it typically employ a staff member whose primary job is to reformat billing data for submission. This is an avoidable expense.

## Time Entry: Capture Rate Determines Revenue

Billing software is only as good as the time that gets into it. The platforms with the highest time-capture rates share common features:

- Mobile time entry that works offline and syncs on reconnect
- Timer functionality that runs within the email client, document editor, and calendar
- AI-assisted time narrative suggestions based on the matter and activity type
- Minimum time increment settings that default to tenths of an hour (0.1h) rather than quarter-hours

The difference between a 0.1h and a 0.25h minimum increment sounds trivial. For a firm billing at $400/hour with 20 fee-earners, it represents a meaningful difference in captured revenue across a year.

## Integration with Your Accounting System

Your billing platform and your accounting system need to speak to each other. The question is how: native integration, API-level integration via a middleware like Zapier or Make, or manual export/import.

**Native integration** (Clio + QuickBooks Online, Cosmolex's built-in accounting, MyCase + QuickBooks) is the most reliable. Trust receipts, operating income, and costs flow automatically, and the chart of accounts mapping is handled once during setup.

**API integration** works well when maintained but introduces a dependency on the middleware provider staying current with both platforms' API changes. We have seen integrations break silently after a platform update — billing records stopped syncing to accounting for weeks before anyone noticed.

**Manual export/import** is not a long-term solution for a firm billing more than a handful of matters per month. The reconciliation overhead compounds quickly.

When evaluating platforms, confirm the specific version of the integration, whether it is bidirectional, how trust transactions are mapped, and who is responsible for maintaining it when one platform updates its API.

## Collecting What You Bill: AR Management Features

The billing platform that sends the invoice is also the right platform to manage collections. Platforms with strong AR management features include:

- Automated payment reminders at configurable intervals (7, 14, 30 days past due)
- Online payment portals that support credit cards and ACH
- Partial payment tracking and payment plan management
- Ageing reports that flag matters where the last payment was received more than 60 days ago

The integration between billing and collections matters because many law firms handle them as separate processes — finance sends the invoice, and then the managing partner chases payment via ad hoc calls or emails with no systematic tracking. The result is that collection effort is concentrated on relationships the partner values, not on the invoices most likely to become bad debt.

## What We Tell Firms That Are Switching Platforms

Platform migrations are more disruptive than firms expect. The historical billing data, the client matter structure, the custom billing codes and rate tables, the trust ledger history — all of it needs to move, and the migration rarely goes cleanly without someone who has done it before.

If you are considering a switch, we would recommend: (1) defining your functional requirements before looking at platforms, (2) getting a written data migration plan from the new vendor that specifies what historical data will and will not migrate, and (3) running parallel systems on a subset of matters for at least 60 days before full cutover.

The platform choice matters less than the implementation. A poorly implemented market-leading platform produces worse outcomes than a well-implemented second-tier one.
